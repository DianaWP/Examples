First Projects

My very first projects when I started learning to code are always going to be special to me. They were pretty basic and probably not done terribly well but they worked and they are the spark that kept me going. The very first project was through my work at the time. The National Archives had seen all these vintage radios popping up and knew that people were able to connect them to the internet somehow but they weren’t really sure how to go about getting something like this done so they came to the AV company where I was working as the Office Coordinator. We mostly installed digital signage and AV systems into schools and government buildings so the original solution was to connect up a device that would play set pieces of the audio which the vintage radio would sit on top of. This would have looked like most museum exhibits where you of course press the big metal button on the outside of the display but the hope was that the radio could take part in a travelling exhibition related to early immigration within Australia.The audio in question had originally played on radio stations and included segments to teach basic conversational English to immigrants.
At this point I asked my boss if I could show him a proof of concept using a raspberry pi and some Pyhton that I had been learning and he agreed. I did get some help from a friend who looked after the electronic side of things and together we went to work. The radio was completely dead so we removed most of the internal workings and replaced them with a small powered speaker and the raspberry pi. I also used jewellery string to fix the dial as I knew you could get specific weights of thread and we fixed the radio so the dials turned to set the station and we hooked up the volume switch to the raspberry pi breadboard. This meant we could program the on/off switch and control the volume. My colleague also set up a little strip of warm LED lights so the station selector would light up as if the radio was working as expected.
At this point I was ready to write the program to play the selected audio files. I started out with a simple tutorial from the Adafruit site which got me a lot of the way but I decided to use the readout from the oscilloscope so we could set specific marked stations to play one file rather than having them play one after the other so you would “dial in” the station and it would start playing a set piece from the audio file.
This was a great first project because whilst the programming aspect was fairly simple I had to solve a lot of specific problems that were unique to the customers requirements and the radio body we were using. Unfortunately as I did this for a previous employer I no longer have access to the final code but I am very proud to know that the National Archives has a display that I worked on.

It Better Not Be Too Late

It is never too late. One question I see and hear a lot is “Am I too old to learn to program” and to that I have to say…. I bloody hope not. I just turned 39 and I am not quite where I want to be just yet, but I am still enjoying the journey. I wont lie and say its not hard and I have certainly loaded up Seek and looked for another office job on occasion, but I can’t. I have discovered programming and the women that do it and I can’t go back now, I’m having too much fun.
I have made some amazing friends through this journey, some if whom I have never even met. The women in tech community have been nothing short of amazing and encouraging every step of the way. I get that it can be overwhelming, especially if, like me, you ever worry about making the wrong decision because let’s face it there is so much to learn. So much you could learn! So much you could do with this newfound skill I mean how is anyone supposed to know where to start and then where to go? And its true, the possibilities are basically endless but to me that’s also what kind of makes it fun.
I was very good in school up to a point but I’ll be honest once I got out of primary school and I was supposed to pick courses that would decide my future I became very disillusioned by being told what to learn. Suddenly my assignments were no longer “pick a native animal and tell us whatever you want about it” but more “Answer this specific question based on what we specifically read in this textbook”.
One of the things I love so far about programming is that you may have a style guide to follow but basically there will be a bunch of ways to get the same result and if you get that result then it’s probably ok and as you learn you will discover other ways that also work but are maybe even better. Another thing that made me fall in love with programming and the tech community is that I can combine it with anything I want. My imagination is the only thing stopping me (which, to be fair is a big roadblock but maybe you have a better imagination than I do).
I also really enjoy how creative it is. I have never been very good at craft and I cant draw to save my life but I always tried different hobbies because I find creative pursuits scratch a very specific itch. The trouble was I never had the time, money or talent to really get where I needed and I would always get frustrated with how much effort I needed to put in to catch up to other people I admired. You know the people that seem to be inherently better at a thing? I know they are the first to tell you that they also find it hard but for some reason programming scratches that same itch and whilst I know I am behind and there is certainly people I admire who will always be better than me, for some reason it matters less.
Maybe its because if I can get my program to do what I want then it is good enough even if someone does it prettier and faster than me or maybe its just because I love it more than I did other crafts. Its still frustrating and I still have major imposter syndrome but for some reason I just cant quit it.

Dynalite to Smart Lights

HTML, CSS and JavaScript

My second ever project when I was just starting out was a fairly simple sounding request. My colleague at work used to install semi smart lights called Dynalite. These were the go to smart light before we figured out you could connect lights to the internet but they were still considered smart because you had a fancy touch panel to control them.
You mostly only saw them in office buildings and the like but as an installer my friend had set up his whole house with them as well.
The trouble with the lights was that you had to be a licensed installer to be able to use the software that was needed to set the lights up and there was no way to control the lights without using the touch panels. Through talking to the manufacturer we did discover however that there had been some investigation on using the internet but at that stage it had been put on the backburner. It did however mean there was a tiny section in the installers guide with some addresses you could connect to. This was not a lot to go off but we decided it was worth a try.
With a little help from a much more experienced software engineer we discovered the addresses were not quite right and were able to find the correct ones and we were off.
I don’t know that I would recommend this as a beginner project as there definitely wasn’t a single tutorial to assist but through a lot of trial and error we figured out that the IP address on my friends home network needed to be static so once that was set up we simply set about using JavaScript to control turning the lights on and off per section as well as setting options to set to 50% and 75% in some rooms using simple OnClick events and Buttons.
With a little HTML and CSS we set up a simple web page with the labelled buttons and from there my friend was able to control his lights simply by using his mobile phone from wherever he was without needing to buy all new lights and panels.

General Assembly Projects

HTML CSS JavaScript React

Studying with General Assembly was a great experience. I did the 10 week part time JavaScript course and I would love to go back for more.
I really enjoyed having a teacher that was very knowledgeable and being able to speak to other people in my cohort live was wonderful. Everyone we very helpful and had different levels of knowledge so you could always get help if you ever got stuck. Each week we basically worked on a new project and quite a bit of time was spent working with connecting to API’s which I found really informative.
The types of projects we worked on included a chat bot with pre selected messages. A Movie Search using the IMDB API as well as a music search using the Spotify API and a Wine app using firebase and API connectors all of which were really interesting and well thought out.
Generally we would start the class with a little starter code and follow along to a tutorial and then for the second day and that weeks homework we would need to add additional features to the project built in class. You could definitely tell which class members had more of a design background as some of the homework results looked amazing but there was never any judgement of those with less artistic talents (aka me).
The final project for the course required us to connect to an API of our choosing and needed to produce output based on user input. We all had to come up with a plan and discuss it with our teacher who would give us feedback and suggestions as well as approval to go ahead with our idea.
My chosen idea was JacketApp which was designed to connect to a weather API that would give you the expected daily temperature from any city you desired. Based on the given temperature the app would then show a jacket that would suit the weather. So if it was rainy and cold you would be shown the image of a parka, if it was cold but dry you would get a faux fur etc through to mid weight jackets and light cardigans until you reached a temp that was warm enough to not need a jacket. I used RapidAPI to connect to the OpenWeather API. This allowed me to get the temp from any city in the world, convert to Celsius and then based on ranges that I set would select an image to show on the website.
I plan to go back to this app and update it with more jacket options and possibly will add a section for shoes and I also wish to rewrite it in .Net. I was of course inspired to work on this app due to my love of jackets and the “All you need is a light Jacket” line from Miss Congeniality. One day I will work out how to build the closet app from Clueless but for now I will start there.

Ultimate API Challenge

HTML CSS JavaScript JSON

Recently I found myself stuck in a bit of a learning rut. I was working on a few ideas that were a bit bigger and as such I had neglected a few of my “I definitely want to go back and refactor this” projects but I just wasn’t motivated by looking at my old code that I had already got working.
So whilst procrastinating on Instagram I came across a wonderful set of tutorials by the wonderful DanaScript. She has built The Ultimate API Challenge and set up a wonderful website where you can get your hands dirty completing the challenges https://theultimateapichallenge.com/challenges.
They have a few different options and you can either load up the starter code from Github and follow along with the provided YouTube videos or you can dive straight in with a few hints and some links to documentation that could come in handy.
These are great for going over connecting to API’s and getting the required data as well as practicing setting up basic web apps over and over. You can use as little or as much guidance as you like and I have found that I enjoy starting off with the tutorial but skipping ahead from time to time and of course making changes to the CSS to make it more my own.
Something about the way they are set out has really helped remind me that I have learned a lot and I can actually build things and troubleshoot as I go. I think they are great for beginners who want to move from the slow and steady hand holding tutorials from say CodeCademy to a slightly more intermediate option but still want to have that person right there to help if you get stuck.
I am currently up to the Lyrics API challenge and I look forward to completing them all.

C# Work

C# .Net

Currently through my role I work mostly with Microsoft Dynamics and whilst most of the programming I have done has been on the JavaScript side I am very keen to learn more about C# and .Net moving forward.
I have done the usual tutorials and recently completed one which required building the game Breakout (basically pong against a wall of bricks instead of an opponent). I have ordered a few text books as well as I really enjoy reading up on new languages as much as I enjoy working with them.
My next plan is to work on an app that I have had in my head for years. In a previous life I worked for a high end jewellery store and part of my role was to write up valuations and photograph jewellery for the purpose of being insured and on the other side I also had to type up the insurance claims when people had lost their jewellery.
When your jewellery is lost or stolen the general process is you go through the store to try and find similar items to what was stolen and those details are provided to the insurance company to then decide how much to give the client. Now obviously this is a lot quicker and easier if you have photos of the item and any specific details and whilst all insurance companies and policies are different I promise they are a lot faster with writing that cheque if we can include photos and specific details. My idea from there is to create a small app where you can photograph your piece and simply select the details such as stone type and size, weight, type of metal etc when it is fresh in your mind so if the worst was to happen, you have it right there with you ready to send off.
Basically after working in a jewellery store I have pieces I would want replaced but even knowing what I know, I do not have every piece recorded so the app is also a great way to motivate me to photograph all my pieces and write down what I have. The best reason to build something.
